Monday

Today after work I go to chess club. In the past I played folks way above me who could effortlessly crush me, thankfully today I find someone who is at my same noobish level and we have a fair 40 minute long match.

Unfortunately I’m still not good enough at chess to really be able to describe it yet but I do know roughly that chess follows an opening, middle, and endgame structure in terms of how folks talk about it so I’ll try to roughly do that here.

Opening:

In the beginning we both move very cautiously. Wherever any of our pieces are at risk we don’t dare do anything aggressive with defense being the name of the game. Occasionally we both falter and lose a couple pieces, him a bit worse than me. At this point I’m up roughly a knight and a couple of pawns. This early lead causes me to lower my guard and use my queen to capture a piece which leads to my queen being captured in turn. It was as brutal as it was swift. My arrogance was immediately punished and my eyebrows leaped up in horror. For a split second all I could do was sit in stunned silence as I was down my most valuable piece.

Middle:

A minute later after regaining my composure I realize that in capturing my queen he left his vulnerable as well and I retaliate. I’m thankful for this not so much for the fact that I took out his queen as much as the fact that now he doesn’t have such a huge piece lead over me. From here we cast aside our previous attitudes and much more aggressively capture pieces in the name of whittling down our opponents options. Theres a saying that to capture a King you must first capture his horse and that drives both of our strategies.

While we each put each other in Check once or twice its nothing serious and the main deciding factor here is eliminating pieces. Its here where I start to gain the advantage as I strip away almost the entirety of his pawns. This is important as pawns run the risk of being promoted if they make it across the board. They aren’t the biggest problem immediately but if left unchecked unexpected issues can arise.

Throughout this phase we both strip each other of most of the valuable pieces with a whole bunch of M.A.D. Eventually we get to a point where one of my pawns can become a queen and I have a few pawns, a new queen, king, and knight against his king, rook, and two pawns. At this point we both think I’m in the lead here but victory isn’t quite mine yet.

Endgame:

Here my main objective is to take out his rook while keeping his pawns from advancing, while his is to scramble to take down a bunch of my pieces and try to force a draw. Eventually I take out the rook which pretty much seals the game. It’s not a question of if I win so much as when I’ll win.

If I remember correctly I think when it looks like victory is guaranteed the opponent is supposed to concede defeat but hes committed to seeing it through to the end which I definitely respect. If I want to win this I need to properly put his King in checkmate, none of this “check” nonsense. Its surprisingly difficult to completely trap his King as somehow there is always some point of escape. Sealing the deal requires cornering him into a progressively narrower part of the board using two queens and my knight. Eventually the deed is done and he truly has no moves left to play and the game is won.

This was the first chess game I won in quite a while so I was quite pleased with the results.

I could have played more but that first game went considerably longer than I thought it would so I decide now is a good time to call it and quit while I’m ahead

Friday

Eid Mubarak to all who celebrate! (Although given the demographics of the newsletter this may just be me here). For those unaware Eid actually occurs twice a year, once to celebrate the end of Ramadan, or in this case to celebrate the end of Hajj. Hajj is a 7 day long pilgrimage to Makkah that Muslims are expected to do once in their lifetimes.

As is traditional for Eid my Dad and I go to the masjid for an Eid prayer. Before the prayer starts the imam gives a rather sassy lecture on people not embracing the traditions of Eid. He notes that nobody from Marin actually went to Hajj and that many people today especially the youth have never actually slaughtered a goat as is customary. Typically a lot of people (like myself), will pay to have one slaughtered in Pakistan or another such country rather than doing it themselves. He tells us a story of some people who raised a goat for 2 months and bonded with it before slaughtering which sounds quite tough but at the same time I respect the heck out of that commitment. He also pointedly tells off some folks having a side conversation just one row behind me. The whole lecture was dripping with “I’m not mad I’m just disappointed” vibes.

After this I go to family lunch and spend some 1:1 time with my Dad. He asks me if I’ve “found anybody yet”, and since I don’t feel like telling him all my escapades from the posters to the speed dating events I just told him that I’m working on it. I briefly try to convince him to try something different from what he normally does since I know given his semi retired state he has the time but sadly this doesn’t quite go anywhere.
