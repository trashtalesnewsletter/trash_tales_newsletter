From my understanding the way it works is there are two methods of gene sequencing (in the scope of this lecture anyway).

First some setup. Lets say you have a segment of DNA and you want to find out its genetic makeup (aka what sequence of (A)denine, (G)uanine, (C)ytosine, (T)hymine its made up of). The first method he brought up is Nanopore sequencing. The way this method works is by voltage analysis. Specifically we take the fragment of DNA in question and pass it through a nanopore. When we do this we can measure the voltage through the pore to understand what the underlying components are. From my understanding I don’t think you can get down to the individual sequence of A, G, C, T here. Instead what you can do is take the voltages and map them to sequences may something like AGGCT or something like that. Whats cool here is that you can actually use ML techniques to learn a model between voltages and the different subsequences. The way he explained this to me reminded me a lot of ML in that if you start from scratch you first bootstrap with either synthetic compounds whose genetic makeup you know or simple sequencing algorithms to give a rough set of initial labels which you can then use as a basis for more nuanced algorithms.

The second algorithm is called Illumina sequencing which works by taking a strand of DNA and dividing it up into chunks of a bunch of base pairs and instead attaching special colored blocks to them. The trick here is that only one colored block can attach to a chunk and that will tell us what the make up of the chunk is. The machine takes a picture and we can do a color analysis to figure out the chunk. One tricky thing here is that this process can be error prone so rather than just doing this once, for each chunk you make a bunch of copies and do this whole process and look at the aggregate to figure out the makeup of the chunk. Apparently the underlying process can be rather noisy and there is an upper bound to how clean it can be meaning no matter what you always have to do this with some redundancy. Here too I’m reminded of ML and its really cool to me to see how gene sequencing uses a lot of the same ideas from ML :D .One of the cons with this method is that you can only do it on shorter base pairs at a time whereas with the Nanopore method you can do this on much longer ones. I think there were some pros to this method but unfortunately I don’t remember them (Illumina: higher accuracy, but shorter reads. Nanopore: generally lower accuracy, but super long reads).
