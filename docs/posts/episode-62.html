Monday

Today after work I continued my 1:1 series and caught up with “The Juggler” at Brenda’s French Soul Food. He told me all about what it's like to do a bikepacking trip in the desert. That’s a life hack right there.

He also told me that if you go to small towns in Nevada everyone knows everyone in really interesting ways. Apparently at the general store there the cashier and someone buying something were both complaining about some woman named Jenny who will leave her kid at their doorsteps at night (I think to go gambling). Also this small town used to have a bunch of donkeys in it until they moved them out which according to “The Juggler” meant they took the one interesting this about this small town and completely got rid of it making it a completely unremarkable small town.

I’m not generally a fan of sea food but the fried shrimp they had there was a banger! He asked me the typical question “What’s been the most unexpected thing you learned during these 1:1s?” Given how some of these have gone I don’t think I can post the actual answer publicly but I am somewhat kicking myself for not asking myself the question until now.

On my walk back home left with nothing to do I decided to stare into the abyss and think about life for a while. One of the first things I thought about was why I feel the need to do the bits I do. I think the answer to that is simply that if I don’t do them I risk feeling bored. Sometimes I find day to day life to be mundane and I like any extra way I can spice it up somehow. I remember reading something as a kid where someone was complaining to their grandfather that they were bored and the grandfather rebuked them saying it was their job to entertain myself and I suppose my bits are my own way of doing just that. Why run when you can run with an air purifier? Why simply put your name down at a restaurant when you can put a name thats so obviously fake everyone knows it is (like nobody is looking at me and thinking my name is Dairy or Wuhan but those are names I’ve put down at restaurants). Of course I wouldn’t say all those bits are gut bustingly hilarious or anything but any little bit I can do to make life that much more interesting feels worth it to me.

From there my thoughts turned to the first impression stuff I’ve been doing. Thanks to my training I have a much better sense of what I should / shouldn’t mention to people when I’m trying to optimize for the perfect first impression. But I thought to myself “Why do I like to introduce myself to people the way that I do?” I think the answer to that comes down to not wanting to seem fake. I’ll take a brief tangent here and talk about something I don’t seldom mention and that is what annoys me in other people. Throughout the training we’ve certainly talked a lot about what *I* do that annoys others but the other way around has largely been untouched for the most part. I think in general I do get annoyed less than average but I will mention something that can do it and that is fakeness.

I remember at a previous job I was at I worked with some salespeople who seemed so obviously fake and it really rubbed me the wrong way. I’ve occasionally met people like that outside of work and whether they were really fake or I just read the situation wrong it irked me in a way that other people seldom do. Like you could straight up insult me and I wouldn’t mind as much because I know you’re being genuine with me. And in turn when I meet other people I want them to see who I really am as much as possible. I like my personality and I don’t want to hide it. I don’t want people to think that I’m something I’m not and that they can trust me. Of course thanks to my training I’ve also learned that even if I intend to be genuine the way I say things off the bat can lead to people getting a different impression of me; I understand that and don’t want to belabor the point here as this is more meant to talk about why I act the way I do and less on whether it is optimal.

As I got home I continued to stare further into the abyss and confront the fact that I really hate feeling bored / lonely and desperately try to keep myself busy to avoid confronting those feelings. I don’t think it’s a stretch to say that I feel like I always need to be doing something and the fact that I didn’t have anything planned for a time when I really should (a Friday night) hit me hard.

I lean on Claude a bit here as I actually find LLMs to be surprisingly good to talk to about this kind of stuff. I asked Claude to ask me some more “Stare into the Abyss” questions and I chatted with it a bit about my life. If anyone is interested, here is the chat. I mentioned this in one of my other newsletters but one of my other fears is what I would be without my humor, what would I really be. One of the themes which I think that chat gets into is that for me time that doesn’t feel “productive” in some way can feel like a moral failure if it goes on for too long. It doesn’t have to be productive in the work sense of course but anything fun or interesting qualifies. I tie myself a lot to whatever outputs I am interested in producing and if I don’t have any then I can feel pointless.

Amongst my many areas of self improvement I’ve outlined I honestly think working on this would probably be one of the best for myself. I don’t want to stop doing interesting things as I genuinely like the things I do but I don’t want to feel as much of a *need* to do them or tie my own moral value to them.

Thanks you all for staring into the abyss with me. If you exit out the hallway to your left you can pick up a complimentary “I stared into Humza’s abyss with him and all I got was this lousy hypothetical t-shirt” t-shirts.

Afterwards we go to a cafe where “Top of the Rope” also teaches me how to knit! I think an indoor hobby would be cool and I’ve never learned before. It turns out if you're me learning to knit can be quite hard. I spend a lot of time learning to make a slip knot as for whatever reason I find it surprisingly hard to wrap my head around. Part of it probably comes from the fact that I’m not a very visual person and have a harder than average time grasping how to do something from a visual demonstration. Usually I need to try a whole bunch to get all the details right and considering it took somewhere between 40 minutes to an hour to learn how to do this knot today was no exception.

Once I learn how to do the knot I learn another basic trick of taking stitches and putting them on the needle which is actually pretty easy. Finally she attempts to teach me another trick of taking stitches on one needle and putting them on another but sadly I haven't made it past here just yet. She did say learning to knit takes a lot of patience and I think I’ll try again with her another time but for now at least I learned how to make a knot so that’s something!

I go home and finally write the newsletter. I decided to prioritize this over going to Mission Run Club considering both the weather and the fact that I really want to get this newsletter out :D.
